Spellstrike!

Spellstrike is a features-light fighting game mostly focused on striking.

It may also have a few spells here and there - my questionable artistic skills leave plenty up to the imagination.

Features

  • Wield both sword and sorcery (some imagination required) to overcome your opponent in fast-paced battles
  • Choose 1 of 4 characters, each with a slightly-different silhouette and more-different playstyle
  • Play with controller, keyboard, or some unsightly combination of the two
  • (Desktop Only) If you're willing to put up with port forwarding, play online with friends far away with rollback netcode!

If you like reading extended descriptions, check out the stuff further down the page. Otherwise, enjoy!

Extended Description for Rambling

Summary for Fighting Game Players

For folks familiar with fighting games, here's a more detailed rundown:

This is a 4-button game (Light, Medium, Heavy, and Special). Every character has, at a minimum, one attack for each Normal button and 2 Special attacks: one when used in neutral (usually defensive), and another when used to cancel after a successful attack (usually offensive).

Characters have unique rules for canceling and chaining attacks, with a few common principles:

  1. Normals can only be canceled into other moves after a successful hit.
  2. Heavy normals cannot be canceled.
  3. Special moves cannot be canceled, though some recover very quickly.

Specials are limited use: players get 5 "charges" at the start of each round. Specials in neutral cost 1 charge, special cancels cost 2 charges. There's no way to replenish charges (in this version of the game, anyway) - once they're gone, characters lose an important defensive tool.

Also very important to note: there's no blocking or jumping. Should be fine, though. Just don't get hit.

Design Notes

Spellstrike was built in the style of traditional 2D fighting games, but with drastically (perhaps commically) large cuts to "standard" features. There's no jumping, no throwing, no crouching, and - most importantly - no blocking. There were two main motiviations: I figured it'd be an interesting design challenge, and I figured that every feature I could cut would reduce the odds I'd accidentally introduce some terrible mistakes while working on a tight schedule.

Overall, I think the project worked out okay. The characters definitely have different strengths and weaknesses, but I think it'd take more playing to really dig into how different their play patterns are.

One of the biggest challenges I faced was related to no blocking & no knockdowns. I think that those states of temporary disadvantage are where a lot of gameplay nuance is concentrated. It's also important how those states give players a different mode of thinking to switch to, a different style of mental puzzle to play with. That variety is important for making the gameplay feel more substantial (and make grinding out long sets easier) and that's something I think this version of the game will have trouble with. We'll see how it shakes out in practice.

There were several interesting technical results of this work. I'm not going to write it up now, but I probably should do a postmortem on how things went for my future reference.
